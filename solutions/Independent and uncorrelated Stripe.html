Say we have two random variables X and Y. What does it mean for X and Y to be independent? What about uncorrelated? Give an example where X and Y are uncorrelated but not independent.
Independent means knowing the value of one random variable does not give you any information about the other.
I.e. $P_{X|Y}(x|y) = P_{X}(x)$
Uncorrelated means that there is no linear relationship between X and Y.
I.e. $\rho = \frac{Cov(X,Y)}{\sqrt{Var(X)Var(Y)}} = 0$ or equivalently $Cov(X,Y) = 0$ The classic example is $X \sim N(0,1)$ and $Y=X^3$.
Clearly knowing $X$ gives you $Y$ so this is not independent.
Or knowing $Y$ changes the distribution of $X$.
We can show that this is uncorrelated by showing $Cov(X,Y)=0$.
$\begin{aligned} Cov(X,Y) &= Cov(X,X^2)\\ &= E[(X-E(X))(X^2-E(X^2))]\\ &= E[X^3]-E[X]E[X^2]\\ &= E[X^3]\\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}x^3p(x)\\ &= \int_{-\infty}^{0}x^3p(x) + \int_{0}^{\infty}x^3p(x)\\ &= -\int_{0}^{\infty}x^3p(x) + \int_{0}^{\infty}x^3p(x) \text{ by Symmetry}\\ &= 0 \end{aligned}$
